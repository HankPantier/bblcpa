## What Indiana's new law changes for workers in 2026

Indiana has passed a measure that excludes qualifying tips and overtime pay from state taxable income, and it applies to the 2026 tax year only. That means servers, bartenders, hourly hospitality staff, and anyone who logs overtime hours could see less of that income counted toward what they owe the state.

Here is the part people miss: this is a one year window, not a permanent change. Unless Indiana extends it, the exemption sits squarely on the 2026 tax year and then it closes. Income earned in 2025 or 2027 follows the regular rules.

The break also has limits on what counts. "Tips" generally means voluntary gratuities reported through your employer, and "overtime" means the premium pay you earn above your standard hourly rate. Bonuses, regular wages, and commissions are not part of this carve out.

If you live or work in Indiana and earn either type of income, this affects what you keep. For everyone else, it is a useful preview of how states are testing tip and overtime relief, and a reminder to read the fine print before counting the savings.

<!-- block: content-prose | variant: standard -->
## How this exemption works at the state level

The exemption operates as a subtraction on your Indiana state return. Your tips and overtime still get earned, reported, and tracked the way they always have. When you file for 2026, the qualifying amounts come off your Indiana taxable income rather than disappearing from your paychecks during the year.

That distinction matters for withholding. State withholding tables do not automatically know that a portion of your pay qualifies for the subtraction, so without an adjustment you may have more withheld during 2026 than you ultimately owe. That is not lost money. It comes back as a larger refund or a smaller balance when you file. Some workers will prefer to adjust their state withholding to keep more in each check, and others will leave it alone and take the refund.

Employers still report tips and overtime through normal payroll, so your records and pay stubs remain the documentation you need. The cleanest move now is simple: keep accurate records of what you earned in tips and overtime through 2026, because those figures drive the subtraction when the return is prepared.

## What this means if you also file a federal return

This is a state level break, full stop. Indiana can change how it taxes your income, but it cannot change how the IRS treats it. Federal tax on tips and overtime stays in effect unless federal law changes it separately.

The common mistake is assuming "no tax on tips and overtime" means owing nothing on that income anywhere. Not the case. You may owe less in Indiana for 2026 while still reporting and paying federal tax on the same dollars. Plan for both returns, not just the one with good news. If you want the savings to land where you expect, treat the state subtraction and your federal liability as two separate calculations.

## Why year-round tax planning matters more than one-year exemptions

A one year rule creates a window that opens and shuts fast. Capture it and you keep more. Miss it and there is no makeup year. The 2026 exemption is exactly that kind of window, and the people who benefit most are the ones who plan for it before the year ends, not the April after.

That is the case for working with a tax advisor year round instead of meeting once at filing time. Decisions about withholding, recordkeeping, and timing happen during the year, while you can still act on them. By the time most people open their tax software, the moves that mattered have already passed. A short conversation in the right month tends to be worth more than hours spent in the wrong one.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Indiana adopts no tax on tips and overtime for 2026 | Brammer, Begnaud, & Lattimore CPAs & Consultants

**Q: Does Indiana's no tax on tips and overtime apply every year?**
A: No. The exemption applies to the 2026 tax year only. Unless Indiana extends the law, tips and overtime earned in 2025 or 2027 follow the regular state rules. Treat 2026 as a one year window and plan to capture the break before it closes.

**Q: Do I still owe federal tax on tips and overtime in 2026?**
A: Yes. This is a state level exemption only. The IRS still taxes tips and overtime unless federal law changes separately. You may owe less in Indiana for 2026 while still reporting and paying federal tax on the same income, so plan for both returns.

**Q: What tips and overtime actually qualify for the Indiana exemption?**
A: Qualifying tips generally means voluntary gratuities reported through your employer, and overtime means the premium pay above your standard hourly rate. Regular wages, bonuses, and commissions do not qualify. Keep accurate records, since those figures drive the subtraction on your 2026 Indiana return.

**Q: Do I need to change my withholding now?**
A: Not necessarily. State withholding tables will not automatically account for the subtraction, so you may have more withheld during 2026 than you owe. That comes back as a refund. Some workers adjust withholding to keep more per check, others take the refund. A CPA can help you decide.
