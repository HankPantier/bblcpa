## The IRS now makes it easier to report tax fraud and scams

The IRS rolled out a streamlined process this year for reporting tax fraud, phishing scams, and identity theft attempts. Instead of digging through multiple forms, taxpayers now have one clear path to flag suspicious activity, whether it's a fake IRS phone call, a spoofed email, or a preparer who promises refunds too good to be true.

For small business owners across Southeast Texas, this matters. Scammers impersonate the IRS constantly, and a streamlined reporting tool means faster action and less confusion about where to send a complaint. If you run a practice, a restaurant, or a farm operation in Port Arthur, knowing how to report fraud quickly protects your business and your employees. Reporting fraud is step one. Knowing what's actually fraudulent, and what to do next, is where a CPA earns their keep.

## Why tax fraud and scams hit small businesses hardest

Fraudsters don't target Fortune 500 companies first. They target the dentist with two office staff, the restaurant owner working the line during dinner rush, the physician's billing office juggling insurance claims, and the agriculture operation with seasonal labor and tight cash flow. Smaller teams mean fewer eyes checking emails, fewer second opinions before wiring money, and less time to catch a fake notice before it does damage.

### Physicians
icon: Stethoscope

Billing offices handle high volumes of payer correspondence, which makes a fraudulent IRS letter easy to miss between insurance paperwork and payroll deadlines.

### Dentists
icon: BadgeCheck

Small administrative staff and frequent EIN-related correspondence make dental practices a common target for fake "verify your information" emails.

### Restaurants
icon: Receipt

Tip reporting, high cash volume, and seasonal staff turnover give scammers more angles, from fake W-2 requests to phishing emails posing as point-of-sale vendors.

### Agriculture Operations
icon: Wallet

Seasonal income, equipment financing, and multiple 1099 contractors create more paperwork trails for scammers to exploit with spoofed notices.

## What to do if you receive a suspicious IRS notice or letter

### Pause before you respond
Don't call the number on the letter, click a link, or wire money. The IRS contacts taxpayers by mail first, almost always, not by phone or text demanding immediate payment.

### Verify the notice independently
Check the notice number against IRS.gov directly, not through any link or number printed on the letter or email itself.

### Save every piece of evidence
Keep the letter, the email headers, voicemail recordings, anything that shows how the attempt arrived and when.

### Call us before you call them
Bring the notice to your CPA first. A short review can tell you whether it's real, and if it's not, how to report it correctly.

### Let Audit Protection take the lead
If it turns out to be a genuine IRS inquiry, our [Audit Protection](https://www.bblcpa.com/what-we-do/audit-protection) service handles the notices, the letters, and the correspondence so you're never on the phone with the IRS alone.
