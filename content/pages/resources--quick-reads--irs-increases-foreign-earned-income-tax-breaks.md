## What the IRS increase in foreign earned income exclusions means for you

Good news if you earn a paycheck overseas: the IRS bumps the foreign earned income exclusion (FEIE) up almost every year to keep pace with inflation. For the 2025 tax year, qualifying taxpayers can exclude up to $130,000 of foreign earnings from U.S. federal income tax, up from $126,500 the year before. That is real money staying in your pocket instead of going to a tax bill.

Here is the catch. The exclusion is not automatic. You have to claim it correctly on a federal return, meet the residency or presence rules, and file on time. Miss a step and the benefit can slip away. Think of the higher limit as a bigger door, but you still have to walk through it the right way to enjoy the savings.

<!-- block: checklist-section | variant: with-image | image: foreign-income-qualification.jpg | alt: "Professional reviewing residency documents and a calendar at a desk" | query: "person reviewing passport documents desk" -->
## Who qualifies for the foreign earned income exclusion

The FEIE rewards people who actually live and work abroad, not folks taking a long vacation. The IRS uses two tests, and you only need to pass one.

### The bona fide residence test
You qualify if you are a U.S. citizen (or resident alien from a treaty country) who has lived in a foreign country for an uninterrupted period that includes a full tax year. This test looks at the nature and intent of your stay, not just the calendar. A physician on a multi-year contract at an overseas hospital often fits here.

### The physical presence test
You qualify if you are physically present in a foreign country for at least 330 full days during any 12-month period. The days do not have to be back to back, but they do have to be counted precisely.

### Southeast Texas professionals who often qualify
We see this come up for Port Arthur area physicians taking international assignments, small business owners running operations across the border, and agricultural operators with overseas contracts or farm interests. If your work takes you out of the country, these tests decide whether the exclusion is yours to claim.

## Foreign earned income, the foreign tax credit, and FBAR filing explained

These three rules get tangled together all the time, but they are separate, with separate deadlines and separate forms.

The **foreign earned income exclusion** lets you leave qualifying wages off your taxable income, up to that $130,000 limit for 2025. It applies to earned income like salary and self-employment pay, not to dividends, interest, or rental income.

The **foreign tax credit** is a different tool. Instead of excluding income, it gives you a dollar-for-dollar credit for income taxes you already paid to a foreign government. In some situations the credit saves you more than the exclusion does, and in some cases you can use both on different slices of income. Picking the right combination takes real math.

**FBAR filing** is not a tax at all. It is a report. If your foreign bank and financial accounts together topped $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file a Foreign Bank Account Report with FinCEN, separate from your tax return. Skipping it carries steep penalties even when no tax is owed.

## Common mistakes that cost taxpayers their exclusion

The exclusion is generous, but it is also unforgiving of paperwork errors. These are the slip-ups we catch most often.

### Forgetting to make the election
icon: FileText

The FEIE only applies if you formally elect it on Form 2555. File without it and the IRS treats your foreign wages as fully taxable, even if you clearly qualified.

### Miscounting your days abroad
icon: Calendar

The physical presence test demands 330 full days. Travel days, layovers, and time in international waters trip people up, and one bad count can sink the whole claim.

### Ignoring FBAR and foreign account reporting
icon: ShieldCheck

Many filers nail the income side and forget the accounts. An unreported foreign account over $10,000 can trigger penalties that dwarf any tax you saved.

### Mixing up earned and unearned income
icon: Coins

The exclusion covers wages and self-employment pay only. Trying to exclude investment or rental income invites an IRS correction and possible interest.

## Frequently Asked Questions About IRS increases foreign earned income tax breaks | Brammer, Begnaud, & Lattimore CPAs & Consultants

**Q: How much foreign income can I exclude for the 2025 tax year?**
A: For the 2025 tax year, qualifying taxpayers can exclude up to $130,000 of foreign earned income from U.S. federal income tax, up from $126,500 in 2024. The IRS adjusts this exclusion for inflation most years, and you must claim it on Form 2555 to receive the benefit.

**Q: Do I qualify for the foreign earned income exclusion?**
A: You qualify by passing either the bona fide residence test, living abroad for an uninterrupted period covering a full tax year, or the physical presence test, spending at least 330 full days in a foreign country during any 12-month period. A Port Arthur CPA can confirm which test fits your situation.

**Q: What is the difference between the FEIE and the foreign tax credit?**
A: The foreign earned income exclusion removes qualifying wages from your taxable income. The foreign tax credit instead gives you a dollar-for-dollar credit for income taxes paid to a foreign government. Depending on your income and tax rates abroad, one may save more than the other, and sometimes both apply.

**Q: Do I have to file an FBAR if I work overseas?**
A: If your combined foreign financial accounts exceeded $10,000 at any point during the year, you must file a Foreign Bank Account Report with FinCEN. FBAR is a separate report from your tax return with its own deadline, and penalties apply even when no tax is owed.
