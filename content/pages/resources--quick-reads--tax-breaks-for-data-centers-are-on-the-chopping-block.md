## What's driving the push to roll back data center tax incentives

Data centers have spent the last twenty years collecting some of the most generous tax breaks state legislatures hand out. Sales tax exemptions on servers and cooling equipment, property tax abatements running ten years or longer, and discounted utility rates have all been standard tools for luring hyperscale facilities owned by companies like Amazon, Microsoft, and Google. That era is getting a second look. Lawmakers in several states introduced bills this year to cap, sunset, or repeal these exemptions outright, arguing the incentives have outgrown their original purpose. AI-driven demand has pushed construction costs and energy use for these facilities to record levels, and the tax breaks attached to them grew just as fast. Legislators facing tight budgets are asking a plain question: are these subsidies still paying for themselves, or did the math stop working somewhere along the way?

<!-- block: content-table -->
## Which states are reconsidering these incentives and what's at stake

At least five states have active legislation addressing data center tax breaks in 2025, and the dollar figures attached to them are substantial.

| State | Status | Scope of rollback | Estimated value at stake |
|---|---|---|---|
| Georgia | Passed Senate, pending House vote | Sunset on the sales tax exemption for new facilities after 2026 | About $135 million annually |
| Virginia | Legislative study commission underway | Review of property tax abatements concentrated in Loudoun County | Roughly $730 million in cumulative exemptions since 2010 |
| South Carolina | Bill introduced in committee | Cap on sales tax exemption duration to 10 years | About $90 million annually |
| Ohio | Committee hearings scheduled | Repeal proposed for facilities under 25 megawatts | Roughly $45 million annually |
| Texas | Interim study ordered | Review of successor-program eligibility for data center projects | Not yet quantified |

None of these bills have reached a governor's desk yet, but the volume of activity signals where the conversation is headed.

<!-- block: content-prose -->
## Why state budgets are pushing back on tech industry tax relief

Three pressures are colliding here. First, several states are staring at post-pandemic revenue shortfalls now that federal relief funds have dried up, and every line item on the books is getting fresh scrutiny. Second, public reporting on the actual size of these exemptions, often buried in economic development filings, has made the dollar figures harder to defend politically. A $730 million exemption draws attention once it's printed in a headline instead of a footnote. Third, and this is the argument doing the most damage, data centers create comparatively few permanent jobs once construction wraps up. A facility that costs a state tens of millions in forgone tax revenue might employ only 50 to 100 full-time workers long-term, a ratio that looks thin next to manufacturing or agriculture incentives offering similar breaks. Lawmakers are weighing that math against constituents asking why their property taxes keep climbing.

## How businesses that rely on data infrastructure could feel the impact

If these tax breaks shrink, the cost doesn't stay with the data center operator. Cloud providers and colocation companies typically pass higher property and equipment taxes through to customers in the form of usage fees, storage rates, or contract renewals. A small business running its point-of-sale system, payroll software, or patient records platform on a major cloud provider may not see a line item that says "tax pass-through," but renewal pricing tends to drift upward when an operator's own costs rise. There's a second-order effect too: companies weighing where to build new capacity may slow expansion in states with less favorable terms, which can affect service reliability and pricing competition over time. Business owners using cloud-based accounting, scheduling, or inventory platforms should watch their software renewal notices closely over the next 12 to 18 months rather than assume pricing stays flat.
