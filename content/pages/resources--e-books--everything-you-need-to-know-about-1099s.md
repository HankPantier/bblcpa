## What a 1099 is and when your business has to issue one

A 1099 is how the IRS tracks money that changes hands outside a regular paycheck. If your business pays an individual, partnership, or unincorporated contractor $600 or more in a calendar year for services, rent, or certain other payments, you're generally required to send that person a 1099 and file a matching copy with the IRS.

The rule sounds simple until you try applying it. A landscaper who cuts your office lawn twice a month? Probably needs one. The freelance designer you paid $400 for a one-time logo? No, that falls under the threshold. The bookkeeper you paid $1,200 to untangle last year's books? Yes, unless they operate as a corporation, which changes the requirement (we'll get into that distinction in the next section).

Vendors who typically trigger a 1099 include contractors, freelancers, attorneys, and landlords you pay rent to directly. The form isn't a penalty or a red flag. It's paperwork the IRS uses to match income a contractor reports against income your business claims paying out. Get the basics right early and the rest of the process stays manageable.

## The 1099 forms small business owners use most

Most Southeast Texas businesses only ever deal with a handful of 1099 variants. Knowing which one applies to a given payment saves you from filing the wrong form, or worse, skipping one entirely.

| Form | Used for | Who typically files it |
|---|---|---|
| 1099-NEC | Payments of $600 or more to non-employee contractors for services rendered | A restaurant paying a freelance sign repair contractor, a dental practice paying a locum dentist |
| 1099-MISC | Rent, legal settlements, prizes, and other miscellaneous business payments of $600 or more | An agriculture operation paying land rent, a physician's office paying an award or prize |
| 1099-INT | Interest payments of $10 or more | A business with a seller-financed note or an interest-bearing client account |
| 1099-K | Payments processed through credit cards or third-party networks like Stripe or Square | Restaurants and retail businesses using a card processor |

NEC handles the bulk of filings for most small business owners, since it covers the contractors, consultants, and service providers businesses hire most often. MISC and INT show up less frequently, and 1099-K is typically issued to you, not by you, if your business accepts card payments.

## How to collect the right information before deadlines hit

The single habit that saves the most January headaches is collecting a signed W-9 from every vendor before you cut the first check, not after. Once a contractor has been paid and moved on, getting them to return a form becomes a chase, and chasing a landscaper or a part-time consultant in the second week of January is nobody's idea of a good start to the year.

Build the habit into your onboarding, not your filing process:

- Request the W-9 the same week you sign a new vendor, before the first invoice is paid
- Keep a running vendor file with entity type, TIN, and mailing address noted for each
- Flag any vendor marked as a corporation, since most corporations are exempt from 1099-NEC
- Confirm the name on the W-9 matches the name on the check, not just the business name on the invoice

A few minutes of paperwork at the start of a relationship beats hours of follow-up emails at the end of the year.

## Key deadlines and what happens if you miss them

1099-NEC forms are due to recipients and to the IRS by January 31. 1099-MISC forms go to recipients by January 31, with an IRS filing deadline of February 28 on paper or March 31 if filed electronically. Miss either deadline, and the penalty scales with how late you are.

| Filed | Penalty per form |
|---|---|
| Within 30 days late | $60 |
| 31 days late through August 1 | $130 |
| After August 1 or not filed at all | $330 |
| Intentional disregard | $660 or more, no maximum |

These penalties are assessed per form, so a business that's late on 15 contractor 1099s is looking at a bill, not a slap on the wrist. The good news: the deadline itself rarely sneaks up on anyone who keeps a vendor file updated through the year rather than reconstructing it in January.

## Common mistakes Southeast Texas business owners make with 1099s

We've reviewed enough year-end filings across Port Arthur and the surrounding area to recognize the same handful of mistakes on repeat. Misclassifying a worker as a contractor when the relationship actually meets the IRS test for an employee is the costliest one, since it can trigger back payroll taxes, not just a missing form.

Other frequent errors are smaller but still add up. Payments made by check often get tracked while payments made on a business credit card get forgotten, even though both count toward the $600 threshold. LLC payees get skipped entirely because owners assume an LLC is automatically exempt the way a corporation is, when in fact a single-member LLC taxed as a sole proprietorship still requires a 1099.

None of these are signs of carelessness. They're what happens when 1099 tracking competes with running a restaurant, a dental practice, or a farm operation during the busiest months of the year.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Small business owner guide to 1099s | Brammer, Begnaud, & Lattimore CPAs & Consultants

**Q: Do I need to send a 1099 to an LLC?**
A: Yes, in most cases. A single-member LLC taxed as a sole proprietorship or a multi-member LLC taxed as a partnership still requires a 1099 if you paid them $600 or more. Only LLCs that elect corporate tax treatment are typically exempt, so check the W-9's entity box before assuming.

**Q: What happens if I file a 1099 late?**
A: Penalties scale with how late the form is, starting at $60 per form within 30 days and rising to $330 per form after August 1, with no cap for intentional disregard. Filing accurately and on time, even a few forms at once, avoids the steepest tier.

**Q: Do I need a 1099 for payments made by credit card?**
A: No. Payments made by credit card, debit card, or through a third-party network like PayPal are reported by the processor on a 1099-K, not by you on a 1099-NEC. Only track cash, check, and direct bank transfer payments toward your own filing threshold.

**Q: When should I collect a W-9 from a new vendor?**
A: Before you issue the first payment, not after. Waiting until January to chase down a vendor's taxpayer ID and entity type turns a five-minute form into a multi-week delay during the busiest filing month of the year.
