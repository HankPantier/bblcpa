## The gap that leaves your financial data exposed

A recent industry survey found that 62 percent of companies now carry cyber liability protection. The other 38 percent are betting they won't get hit. If you run a restaurant, a dental practice, or a family farm in Southeast Texas, it's easy to assume your business is too small to bother with. Criminals see it differently. Small operations often keep weaker defenses and the same valuable records as larger firms, which makes them easier money, not lower priority.

Here in Port Arthur, a single breach can freeze payroll, expose client tax data, and stall your books for weeks. The point isn't to scare you off your morning coffee. It's to close a gap that more than half of businesses have already addressed. Knowing where you stand is the first step toward fixing it.

## What cybersecurity experts say every business must have in place

The protection that 62 percent of companies have adopted is cyber liability coverage, paired with the everyday safeguards that make it work. Insurance alone won't stop an attack. It cushions the financial blow when one gets through, covering things like data recovery, legal costs, customer notification, and the income you lose while systems are down.

But a policy is only as strong as the habits behind it. Experts point to a short, practical list every business should have:

- Multi-factor authentication on email, banking, and accounting logins, so a stolen password isn't enough to get in
- Encrypted storage for payroll, tax, and bookkeeping files instead of spreadsheets on a shared drive
- Regular software updates, since most breaches exploit holes that were already patched
- A clear plan for who does what the moment something looks wrong

None of this requires a technical background. It requires deciding the risk is real and acting before something forces your hand. Most owners already lock their front door at night. This is the same instinct, applied to the data that runs the business.

## Why your financial records are a prime target

Criminals follow the value, and few files hold more of it than your accounting records. Payroll data carries Social Security numbers, bank routing details, and direct deposit information for every employee. Tax filings include EINs, prior-year income, and the kind of identity details used to file fraudulent returns. Bookkeeping records map your vendors, your cash flow, and the exact accounts where money moves.

Put together, that's a complete blueprint of your finances. A thief who gets in can redirect a payroll run, invoice your customers from a spoofed account, or sell your client list on the open market. For a physician's office or a small retailer, the damage rarely stops at the dollars taken. A breach involving patient or customer data can trigger reporting obligations and erode the trust you spent years building in Jefferson County. The records that keep your business running are the same records that make you worth attacking, which is exactly why they deserve real protection.
