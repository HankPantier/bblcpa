## Why financial statements matter for your business

Your financial statements are the scoreboard. Every decision you make, hiring a second nurse, adding a Saturday dinner shift, buying a new tractor, shows up in these three documents before it shows up in your bank account. If numbers make you tense up, you are in good company. Plenty of Southeast Texas business owners would rather do almost anything than open a profit and loss report.

Here is the good news. Reading a financial statement is not a skill reserved for accountants. It is closer to reading a fuel gauge than solving a calculus problem. You need to know what each section is telling you and when to act on it. Knowing your numbers is not optional if you want to grow. It is also not nearly as hard as it looks once someone shows you the map.

<!-- block: content-split | variant: image-right | image: balance-sheet-review.jpg | alt: "Business owner reviewing a balance sheet with an accountant at a desk" | query: "small business owner reviewing financials" -->
## The balance sheet shows where your business stands right now

Think of the balance sheet as a photograph. It captures what your business owns and owes on one specific day. Three parts make up the picture.

Assets are what you own. Cash in the bank, money customers owe you, your espresso machines, your dental chairs, your delivery truck. Liabilities are what you owe. Vendor bills, the equipment loan, the line of credit you tapped to cover payroll in a slow month. Equity is what is left over when you subtract what you owe from what you own. That number is your stake in the business.

The whole thing balances by design: assets always equal liabilities plus equity. If you sold everything and paid off every debt today, equity is the cash you would walk away with.

Why does this matter to a restaurant owner or a physician? Because the balance sheet tells you whether you can cover next quarter, whether you are carrying too much debt, and whether the business is building real value or just spinning its wheels. It is the first place a lender or a buyer looks.

<!-- block: content-split | variant: image-left | image: income-statement-restaurant.jpg | alt: "Restaurant owner checking monthly sales figures on a tablet in a dining room" | query: "restaurant owner reviewing sales reports" -->
## The income statement tells you whether the business is working

If the balance sheet is a photograph, the income statement is a highlight reel. It covers a stretch of time, a month, a quarter, a year, and answers one question: did you make money?

Start at the top with revenue, the total you brought in from sales. Subtract cost of goods sold, the direct cost of what you sold. For a restaurant that is food and the line cooks. For a dental practice it is the crowns and the lab fees. What remains is gross profit, the money left to run everything else.

Next come operating expenses: rent, insurance, marketing, office staff, software. Subtract those and you reach net income, the bottom line. Positive means you profited. Negative means you spent more than you earned.

Watch gross profit margin month to month. A slow slide there often signals a pricing or cost problem long before the bottom line screams about it. With our secure online platform, you see these trends in real time instead of finding out three months later when the books finally close.

## The cash flow statement reveals the truth about your money

Here is the part that surprises people. You can post a profit on your income statement and still bounce a payroll check. Profit and cash are not the same thing, and the cash flow statement is where the difference shows up.

A restaurant might book a big catering sale in March but not collect until May. The profit shows in March. The cash does not. Meanwhile rent is due now. The cash flow statement tracks the actual movement of dollars in and out, sorted into three buckets.

Operating activities cover the day to day: collecting from customers, paying suppliers and staff. Investing activities cover big purchases or sales, like a new ultrasound machine or selling off old equipment. Financing activities cover loans, repayments, and money you put in or take out.

When operating cash is consistently negative while the income statement looks healthy, something is off, and it usually means money is trapped in unpaid invoices or inventory. This is exactly the kind of warning sign that is easy to miss on your own and obvious to a professional reviewing your numbers every month.

## How to read these three statements together

No single statement tells the whole story. Read together, they cross check each other. The net income on your income statement flows into equity on your balance sheet and sits at the top of your cash flow statement. When those numbers line up, the picture holds.

When they do not, pay attention. Rising profit but shrinking cash points to collection or inventory trouble. Growing equity with mounting debt means you are borrowing to look healthy. Strong sales with thin gross margins means you are busy but not building.

You do not need to become an accountant to spot these patterns. You need enough fluency to ask your CPA the right questions and understand the answers. That informed conversation is where good decisions start.

## Frequently Asked Questions About How to read a financial statement | Brammer, Begnaud, & Lattimore CPAs & Consultants

**Q: What are the three main financial statements?**
A: The three main financial statements are the balance sheet, the income statement, and the cash flow statement. The balance sheet shows what you own and owe on a single day, the income statement shows profit over a period, and the cash flow statement tracks the actual dollars moving in and out of your business.

**Q: Why can a business show a profit but still run out of cash?**
A: Profit and cash are not the same. You can record a sale and post a profit on your income statement before the customer actually pays you. Meanwhile, rent and payroll are due now. The cash flow statement captures this timing gap, which is why a profitable business can still struggle to cover its bills.

**Q: What should a small business owner watch on the income statement?**
A: Watch gross profit margin month to month. A steady decline there usually signals a pricing or cost problem before it reaches the bottom line. Also track revenue trends and how quickly operating expenses are climbing relative to sales, so you catch issues early instead of at year end.

**Q: Do I need an accountant to read my financial statements?**
A: You can learn enough to understand the basics and ask informed questions. A seasoned CPA team adds value by reviewing your numbers every month, cross checking the three statements, and flagging warning signs you might miss.
