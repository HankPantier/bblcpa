## The survey numbers worth paying attention to

A recent workplace survey found that 46% of Gen Z employees believe regular use of AI tools is making them less intelligent, less creative, or worse at problem-solving. That's not a fringe opinion. It's nearly half of the youngest generation in the American workforce raising their own hand and saying something feels off.

The concern isn't really about AI being bad. It's about what happens when a tool built to save time on rote tasks starts replacing the thinking that used to go along with those tasks. Drafting an email is one thing. Reasoning through a cash flow problem, a hiring decision, or a tax strategy is another. For a related take, see [Americans split on using AI for personal finances](/resources/quick-reads/americans-split-on-using-ai-for-personal-finances).

For small business owners across Southeast Texas, the finding is worth a second look. Not because AI is the enemy, but because the habits forming in today's workforce eventually show up in how decisions get made, including financial ones.

## Why this matters for small business owners right now

Most small business owners didn't grow up using AI for bookkeeping, but plenty are starting to lean on it for quick answers: categorizing an expense, drafting a budget, even interpreting a profit and loss statement through [outsourced accounting](/what-we-do/outsourced-accounting) tools. Used well, that's efficient. Used carelessly, it's a problem.

AI tools are good at pattern matching. They are not good at knowing your business. They don't know that your restaurant's slow Tuesdays are seasonal, not a trend. They don't know that the dental practice you're running has equipment financing coming due in eight months, or that your agricultural operation just had a year with unusual commodity swings.

When a business owner asks a chatbot whether it was a good month instead of asking someone who actually understands the business, financial judgment gets outsourced to a tool with no skin in the game and no liability if it's wrong. That's the real risk in the Gen Z survey results: not that AI gets used, but that human judgment quietly fades while nobody's watching the slope of the decline.

Decisions about pricing, payroll, taxes, and growth deserve a second set of eyes that can push back, not just autocomplete.

## AI is a tool, not a financial advisor

AI has real, practical uses in a small business. It can summarize a long document, draft a first version of a policy, or flag an unusual transaction worth a closer look. None of that is in question.

What AI can't do is sit across the table and ask why your margins dropped in March, or know that your entity structure made sense in 2019 and stopped making sense after last year's growth. It can't represent you in front of the IRS. It can't weigh your specific tax situation against Texas rules and tell you what actually saves money.

Tax planning, entity selection, and cash flow oversight require judgment built on relationships and years of pattern recognition, not just pattern matching. A CPA who has worked with restaurants, physicians, and agriculture businesses across Southeast Texas for decades brings context an algorithm can't access: your specific numbers, your specific goals, and what's realistic for your business this year. That's the thinking behind [CFO-level business management guidance](/what-we-do/business-management-services) at a fraction of an executive's cost.

## How to use AI at work without losing your financial edge

AI can be useful at work without becoming a crutch. The business owners who stay financially sharp tend to share a few habits, and you can see how other owners are approaching this in [SMB owners are happy and advancing with AI](/resources/quick-reads/smb-owners-are-happy-and-advancing-with-ai-resilience-and-autonomy):

- Use AI to draft, not to decide. Let it write a first pass on an email or summarize a report, then review it with your own judgment before it goes anywhere.
- Ask why, not just what. If a tool gives you an answer, ask it to explain its reasoning, then check that reasoning against what you actually know about your business.
- Keep a human checkpoint on anything with a dollar sign attached. Payroll, tax filings, and pricing decisions should pass through a person who's accountable for the outcome.
- Review your own financials monthly. A real-time dashboard helps, but the habit of actually looking at the numbers is what keeps your instincts sharp.
- Call your CPA, not a chatbot, about anything involving the IRS, an entity change, or a five-figure decision.

None of this means avoiding AI. It means staying the one in charge of it.
